UK Open Banking roadmap plugs opportunity gap for banks with non-sweeping VRP

News17-05-2023by Rajeeb Gurung, Contentive

As the UK looks ahead to the next phase of open banking, regulators and industry contemplate ways to close gaps and encourage innovation

The Joint Regulatory Oversight Committee (JROC) has published a recommendation for the future of open banking in the UK, which includes an exploration of the business use case with the implementation of non-sweeping variable recurring payments.

The recommendation is intended to prioritise sustainability and competitiveness, unlocking the wider potential for payments and building a scalable model for future data-sharing propositions.

Wasim Mushtaq, managing director of 1Consulting Group, who had been calling for clarity on the future of open banking during his time as transformation lead at Standard Chartered, called it a clear and strong roadmap focusing on the right priorities. 

The current state of open banking

The UK’s regulation-led approach has positioned it as the European leader in open banking. Industry experts have argued that much of its success and speed of adoption is due to industry-wide cooperation between regulators, financial firms and fintech companies.

Under the mandate, the UK’s major banks were instructed to give third parties access to customer data which drove innovation in the open banking space.

In January 2023, the Open Banking Implementation Entity (OBIE) marked the completion of the CMA open banking roadmap, five years after its inception. At the time of the announcement, the UK had registered 6.5m active users of open banking-enabled products. In February, the user base reached 7m.

Although the CMA Open Banking Roadmap delivered on its goals, some industry experts see it as a lopsided strategy largely favouring fintech companies.

Third-party companies have benefited from access to the banks’ customer data, enabling them to launch innovative and customer-centric services.

According to the Open Banking Impact Report by Open Banking Limited (OBL), 173 firms offered services as agents of regulated TPPs at the end of 2022, an increase of 33 since March that year.

Banks, on the other hand, have allocated significant amounts of investment on upgrading their legacy systems to enable third parties to connect to their APIs. The CMA9, the nine largest UK banks mandated by the CMA to implement open banking, reportedly expended them £81m on the project, via the OBIE, just in 2019.

Conflicting visions of the future

Contradicting views have emerged among the participants on the future of open banking.

Open banking’s Strategic Working Group report, which would support JROC on the development of the roadmap for the future direction of open banking, also reported on the differences despite finding that participants had the desire for open banking to work well.

It noted, “Large ASPSPs often cited the lack of a business case for further developments whilst TPPs tend to advocate for them.”

Commercialisation comes into focus

JROC’s recommendation for the future of open banking seeks to tackle commercialisation issues for banks. 

“In order to encourage competition and innovation in a more sustainable manner, the ecosystem must encourage and facilitate new commercial agreements to emerge between participants, in which data holders can charge for access to premium APIs,” the committee states. 

JROC’s focus on commercialisation is welcome news to all participants.

Mushtaq says, “I am actually quite happy to see that there is a bigger focus on commercialization, not just for the TPPs, but also for the firms, such as the banks, providing the data.”

“The ecosystem requires a larger infrastructure, better reliability, more APIs to monitor and further technology resources to support wider teams. So, there needs to be a more sustainable and long-term view of providing and paying for that across all partners - not just the banks. So, I welcome that overall thinking. ”

Kush Shah, senior product manager at Bottomline, also welcomes the commercial opportunity for banks.

Shah calls for a fairer ecosystem where all participants will be able to benefit from the innovation whilst collaborating and contributing to the development.

“Previously, some businesses were not able to monetise or receive any revenue due to how it was implemented, which I think was the foundation that was very much needed. Whether it was fair or not is a separate question. When it comes to moving forward, if we are to work together as an industry, it must be commercially attractive for everyone. Otherwise, we will end up in the same situation again, where only the minimum gets achieved, which does not enable things like availability, scalability, performance, or how well the services are delivered. So, there has to be an incentive for everyone involved at the end of the day.”

Non-sweeping VRP is the first commercial opportunity in open banking

One of the commercial opportunities JROC is exploring includes non-sweeping variable recurring payments (VRP).

Some banks have already implemented VRP, and some of these initiatives stand as examples of productive collaborations between financial and fintech firms.

In May 2022, Natwest partnered with payment providers TrueLayer, GoCardless and Crezco to offer VRP as a payment option for businesses and consumers.

JROC, meanwhile, is focusing on the non-sweeping use cases, which according to Mushtaq have the potential to boost open banking users.

He says, "Many of the interesting use cases for innovation are in the non-sweeping areas for VRP. For example, you can have one-click purchases in e-commerce. You could have one-click purchases for subscriptions or anything else as soon as you enable widespread account-to-account payments.  I think that’s when you’ll see exponential growth across open banking user numbers, because it’s more accessible and inclusive for everyone. What we’ve seen across most industries is that customers are willing to pay a premium for convenience.”

The commercialisation opportunity for each participant is welcome, but challenges remain upon closer inspection.

While JROC acknowledges support from the open banking participants for VRP, it also highlights the disagreement on the implementation process and whether it should be a regulatory (free or not) or industry-led initiative.

As the industry awaits clarity on the funding model, Giles Rowlinson, senior payments manager EMEA at Flywire, warns that getting it wrong could hinder consumer uptake

He says, “VRP will be the first opportunity to charge for open banking. If that is set too high, we will see a limited uptake, with people continuing the use the existing rails.”

He explains: “If the pricing is set the same as a card, it could create real obstacles. I am not sure if the benefit is sufficient to offset a high fee. I think it has to be a relatively cheap and cost-effective payment mechanism.”

“Most of the fintechs that have been born out of open banking are used to free transactions. I think we will see major problems if we start to see high fees built into that.”
